# Retrieval & Faithfulness Evaluation — Results

Generated by `retrieval_eval.py` over the 42-paper neuroscience corpus indexed in
Chroma (3,492 chunks, 1000 chars / 200 overlap, `all-MiniLM-L6-v2` embeddings).
10 questions, each with a hand-labeled gold snippet (a verbatim phrase from the
single passage that answers it). Candidate pool = 30 chunks/query.

This evaluation is **separate from `evaluate.py`**, which grades answer quality.
Here we grade (1) retrieval and (2) faithfulness.

---

## 1. Retrieval quality — cross-encoder reranker, before vs after

**Reranker:** `cross-encoder/ms-marco-MiniLM-L-6-v2`. The base retriever pulls 30
candidates; the reranker rescoring every `(query, chunk)` pair and resorts them.
Because reranking only reorders the pool, reranked recall@k can never exceed the
base retriever's recall@30 (here **0.40** chunk-level).

### Primary metric — chunk-level (did we retrieve the exact gold passage?)

A hit means the specific gold answer-passage was retrieved.

| Retriever | recall@1 | recall@3 | recall@5 | recall@10 | MRR |
|-----------|:--------:|:--------:|:--------:|:---------:|:------:|
| base      | 0.00 | 0.10 | 0.10 | 0.20 | 0.071 |
| reranked  | 0.10 | 0.10 | 0.20 | 0.30 | 0.144 |

The reranker roughly **doubles MRR (0.071 → 0.144)** and lifts recall@1, @5, @10.
Gains are modest because most failures are not ranking failures — the gold passage
is simply absent from the 30-candidate pool (see the diagnostic below).

### Per-question rank of the gold chunk (base → reranked)

| Q | topic | base | reranked |
|---|-------|:----:|:--------:|
| 1 | Waddington / definition        | 7  | 7  |
| 2 | DNA methylation                | 28 | **4** |
| 3 | epitranscriptome / m6A         | –  | –  |
| 4 | activity / demethylation       | –  | –  |
| 5 | marks required for memory      | –  | –  |
| 6 | maternal care / stress         | –  | –  |
| 7 | depression / antidepressant    | 29 | **22** |
| 8 | addiction                      | 2  | **1**  |
| 9 | Nugent 2015 sex differentiation| –  | –  |
| 10| transgenerational inheritance  | –  | –  |

(`–` = the gold chunk was not among the 30 candidates, so the reranker cannot
recover it.) Where the gold chunk *was* in the pool, the reranker either held or
improved its rank in every case (most dramatically Q2: 28 → 4).

## 2. Reading the baseline — why recall@10 is only 0.20

recall@10 is low, which per the rubric means "the right chunk is often not
retrieved at all," and a reranker cannot fix that. But splitting the misses by a
**lenient paper-level metric** (was *any* chunk from the gold's home paper
retrieved?) shows two distinct problems:

### Diagnostic metric — paper-level (was the right *document* found?)

| Retriever | recall@1 | recall@3 | recall@5 | recall@10 | MRR |
|-----------|:--------:|:--------:|:--------:|:---------:|:------:|
| base      | 0.10 | 0.50 | 0.50 | 0.60 | 0.305 |
| reranked  | 0.30 | 0.30 | 0.60 | 0.70 | 0.379 |

chunk-level recall@10 = **0.20** vs paper-level recall@10 = **0.60**. The gap means:

- **4/10 — true retrieval misses** (Q2 borderline, Q3, Q4, Q10): the home paper is
  not even in the top 10. For Q3 ("What is the epitranscriptome…") the top hits are
  all the *Greally* "what is epigenetics" review, not the m6A paper — the
  definitional phrasing embeds near general reviews, not the specific result. A
  reranker cannot help these; this points at embeddings / chunking / retrieval
  depth, or query rewriting.
- **4/10 — right paper, wrong chunk** (Q5, Q6, Q7, Q9): the home paper is retrieved
  at rank 2–3, but the one gold chunk ranks deep (54, 102, 29, 87). These are
  ranking failures *within the right document* — exactly what the reranker targets,
  and also a sign the single-passage gold is strict (a different, equally valid
  passage may be answering the question).

Note the reranker improves paper-level recall@1 (0.10 → 0.30) but *drops* recall@3
(0.50 → 0.30): optimizing query–chunk relevance does not always keep the gold's
home paper high, so the cross-encoder is not a uniform win at every cutoff.

## 3. Faithfulness — is every claim grounded in the retrieved context?

A **separate** judge call from the answer-quality judge in `evaluate.py`. For each
question it takes the retrieved context and the pipeline's generated answer and
asks whether *every* factual claim is supported by that context.

**Faithful answers: 8 / 10 (80%).** Two answers contained unsupported claims:

- **Q7 (depression / antidepressant action)** — the answer attributes specific
  findings to *Peña & Nestler (2018)* that the retrieved context only lists as
  citations to other papers (Cruceanu et al. 2013, Golden et al. 2013). The model
  turned "the review cites X" into "the review found X."
- **Q9 (Nugent 2015 sex differentiation)** — the answer adds interpretive framing
  ("challenges earlier assumptions about active vs. passive default," "becoming
  female requires active epigenetic suppression of male programming") that goes
  beyond what the retrieved context literally states.

Both failures are **over-attribution / over-interpretation**, not invented facts —
the kind of subtle unfaithfulness a grounded-claims judge is meant to surface.

### Prompt fix (implemented in `ask.py`)

The generation prompt was tightened with three rules: use only what the context
states (don't fill gaps from general knowledge); add no interpretation or framing
the context doesn't state; and credit a finding to a paper only if the context
shows that paper made it (a reference cited *inside* a paper is not that paper's
own result). Measured controlled, same session:

| prompt | faithfulness | answer quality (`evaluate.py`) |
|--------|:------------:|:------------------------------:|
| original  | 8 / 10 | 4 / 10 |
| tightened | **10 / 10** | 5 / 10 |

Faithfulness reached **10/10** and both prior failures (Q7, Q9) are fixed, with **no
quality regression** (4 → 5 is within run-to-run noise on 10 questions). The quality
score is low for *both* prompts because retrieval — not generation — is the
bottleneck (see Part 2): on a retrieval-miss question the honest answer is "I don't
know," which the quality judge counts as a miss but which is correctly faithful. In
other words, the original prompt's apparent quality was partly propped up by
answering from the model's own knowledge rather than the retrieved context.

## 4. Stronger embeddings — attacking the retrieval bottleneck (`embedding_experiment.py`)

Part 2 pinned the bottleneck on the embedding model, so we re-embedded the *same*
chunks with **`BAAI/bge-small-en-v1.5`** (same 384-d size as the shipped
`all-MiniLM-L6-v2`, but far stronger on retrieval benchmarks) into a separate Chroma
collection. Same chunks, questions, gold, and reranker — only the embedding changes.

### Chunk-level recall@k / MRR (exact gold passage)

| config | R@1 | R@3 | R@5 | R@10 | MRR |
|--------|:--:|:--:|:--:|:---:|:---:|
| MiniLM base     | 0.00 | 0.10 | 0.10 | 0.20 | 0.071 |
| MiniLM + rerank | 0.10 | 0.10 | 0.20 | 0.30 | 0.144 |
| **BGE base**    | 0.10 | 0.20 | 0.30 | **0.40** | **0.199** |
| BGE + rerank    | 0.10 | 0.10 | 0.20 | 0.30 | 0.150 |

### Paper-level recall@k / MRR (right document found at all)

| config | R@1 | R@3 | R@5 | R@10 | MRR |
|--------|:--:|:--:|:--:|:---:|:---:|
| MiniLM base     | 0.10 | 0.50 | 0.50 | 0.60 | 0.305 |
| MiniLM + rerank | 0.30 | 0.30 | 0.60 | 0.70 | 0.379 |
| **BGE base**    | 0.30 | 0.70 | 0.90 | **0.90** | **0.507** |
| BGE + rerank    | 0.40 | 0.40 | 0.70 | 0.90 | 0.503 |

**Two findings:**

1. **The embedding swap is the real fix — bigger than the reranker.** BGE doubles
   chunk-level recall@10 (0.20 → 0.40) and lifts paper-level recall@10 from 0.60 to
   **0.90** (recall@5 0.50 → 0.90). It rescues the *true misses* a reranker never
   could: Q3 ("epitranscriptome") went from not-in-top-300 to **rank 6** — BGE bridges
   the "epitranscriptome ↔ m6A / N6-methyladenosine" vocabulary gap that MiniLM
   couldn't — and Q10 (transgenerational / Dias) entered the pool at rank 27.

2. **A weak reranker helps a weak retriever but hurts a strong one.** The same
   cross-encoder that improved MiniLM (Q2 28 → 4) *degraded* BGE's already-good
   ranking (Q1 5 → 9, Q3 6 → 16, Q7 12 → 22), dropping chunk-level recall@10 from 0.40
   to 0.30. `ms-marco-MiniLM-L-6-v2` is trained on web passages, not scientific text,
   so on BGE's already-relevant candidates its scores are noisier than BGE's own.

**Bottom line:** upgrade the embedding model (the bottleneck), and do *not* stack this
reranker on top of it — drop it, or use a stronger / domain-matched cross-encoder.
Paper-level recall@10 of 0.90 means the right document now reaches the LLM for 9/10
questions.

### End-to-end payoff (app switched to BGE)

`ask.py` now defaults to the BGE index (`RAG_EMBEDDING=minilm` falls back to the
original). Re-running the full pipeline, holding the tightened prompt fixed and
changing only the embedding model:

| pipeline | answer quality (`evaluate.py`) | faithfulness |
|----------|:------------------------------:|:------------:|
| MiniLM + tightened prompt | 5 / 10 | 10 / 10 |
| **BGE + tightened prompt** | **7 / 10** | **10 / 10** |

Better retrieval converted directly into better answers (**+2/10**), with faithfulness
holding at **10/10** — the system answers more questions correctly *and* stays grounded
(Q3 and Q10 flipped to PASS). The full session arc: the prompt fix took faithfulness
8 → 10/10 at no quality cost (§3); the embedding upgrade took quality 5 → 7/10 at no
faithfulness cost (§4). The 3 remaining quality misses (Q4, Q5, Q6) are **multi-paper
questions** where one of the two needed papers still isn't retrieved (e.g. Q6 gets the
Weaver rat study but not the McGowan human study) — candidates for `bge-base`, query
rewriting, or ensuring multiple source papers are represented in the pool.

## Caveats

- Gold snippets are **hand-labeled** and pin a single passage per question. Several
  questions are answered by more than one paper, so chunk-level recall is a strict
  lower bound — confirm the gold phrases (see `explore_gold.py` for re-labeling).
- The faithfulness judge is itself an LLM (`claude-sonnet-4-6`); its verdicts are
  defensible but, as the Q9 verdict shows, can be pedantic about interpretive
  language.

## Suggested next steps (supported by these numbers)

1. **Address the true misses first** — the bottleneck is retrieval, not ranking.
   VALIDATED in §4: swapping to `bge-small-en-v1.5` lifted paper-level recall@10 from
   0.60 to 0.90 and rescued the true-miss questions. Next: point the app at the BGE
   index and re-run `evaluate.py`; try `bge-base` or query rewriting for the last few.
2. **Use the reranker only with the weak retriever** — it helps MiniLM but *hurts*
   BGE (§4). With a stronger embedding model, drop it or swap in a domain-matched
   cross-encoder.
3. **Tighten the answer prompt** — DONE (see §3). Faithfulness 8 → 10/10 with no
   quality regression; curbs over-attribution (Q7) and interpretive overreach (Q9).
